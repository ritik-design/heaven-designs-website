## What Is a Solar Feasibility Study?

A solar feasibility study is a comprehensive preliminary assessment that evaluates whether a proposed solar project is technically viable, financially sound, and practically executable. Unlike detailed engineering design, which provides construction-ready drawings and specifications, a feasibility study answers the fundamental question: “Should we proceed with this project?”

For solar EPC companies in India, commissioning a feasibility study before investing in detailed engineering serves multiple critical purposes. It validates site suitability, estimates energy generation potential, assesses grid connectivity options, and provides preliminary financial projections. This early-stage analysis typically takes 7-14 days and costs a fraction of full detailed engineering, yet it can save lakhs of rupees by identifying deal-breakers early.

The key distinction between a feasibility study and detailed design lies in depth and purpose. A feasibility study provides a high-level assessment with preliminary layouts and estimates, helping stakeholders make go/no-go decisions. Detailed engineering, on the other hand, delivers construction-ready documentation with precise specifications, structural calculations, and permit-ready drawings. Think of the feasibility study as the reconnaissance mission that determines if the battle is worth fighting.

In the project lifecycle, the feasibility study sits at the very beginning, immediately after initial site identification. It precedes land acquisition finalization, detailed engineering, procurement, and construction. This strategic positioning allows developers to abandon unsuitable sites before significant capital commitment, making it one of the most cost-effective risk mitigation tools available to solar EPCs.

## Key Components of a Solar Feasibility Study

A comprehensive solar feasibility study encompasses multiple technical disciplines, each contributing essential data to the overall viability assessment. Understanding these components helps EPC companies know what to expect when commissioning studies and how to evaluate the thoroughness of deliverables.

### Site Assessment and Land Analysis

The foundation of any feasibility study begins with detailed site assessment and land analysis. Engineers evaluate the total land area available and calculate usable space after accounting for setbacks, access roads, and unusable zones. For ground-mount projects in India, the general rule suggests approximately 4-5 acres per MW for fixed-tilt systems, though this varies based on terrain and layout optimization.

Terrain evaluation examines slope characteristics, as excessive gradients increase civil work costs and may render sites impractical. Most solar installations prefer slopes under 5 degrees, though steeper terrain can be accommodated with additional grading and foundation work. The feasibility study quantifies these requirements and their cost implications.

Access roads and existing infrastructure receive careful scrutiny. Sites must accommodate heavy equipment during construction and maintenance vehicles throughout the project’s 25-year lifespan. Remote locations may require significant access road development, impacting project economics. Environmental and ecological considerations, including protected species habitats, wetlands, and forest land classifications, are documented to identify potential regulatory hurdles.

Land ownership verification, while primarily a legal function, forms part of the site assessment. The study confirms whether land titles are clear, identifies any encumbrances, and notes if multiple parcels require consolidation. These factors directly impact project timeline and feasibility.

### Shading Studies and Energy Yield Analysis

Shading analysis represents one of the most critical technical components of a solar feasibility study. Engineers conduct both near-shading analysis (from on-site obstacles like buildings, trees, or terrain features) and far-shading analysis (from distant mountains, tall structures, or vegetation). Advanced software tools simulate sun paths throughout the year, calculating shading losses for each hour of operation.

In India, where seasonal variations affect sun angles significantly, the feasibility study must account for monsoon cloud cover patterns, winter sun angles, and summer peak production periods. Shading losses can range from negligible (under 2%) for ideal sites to prohibitive (over 15%) for heavily obstructed locations. The study quantifies these losses and recommends mitigation strategies, such as selective tree removal or layout optimization.

Energy yield analysis combines solar resource data with shading calculations to project annual generation. Using historical irradiation data from sources like NASA POWER, NREL, or ground-based monitoring stations, engineers estimate the site’s solar potential. Performance ratio (PR) assumptions, typically 75-85% for well-designed systems in India, account for system losses including inverter efficiency, soiling, temperature effects, and transmission losses.

### Grid Connectivity Assessment

Grid connectivity often determines project viability, particularly for utility-scale installations. The feasibility study identifies the nearest substation, measures the distance, and assesses available evacuation capacity. In many Indian states, grid congestion limits new solar connections, making this assessment critical.

Engineers evaluate appropriate voltage levels based on project size. Small rooftop systems typically connect at 415V or 11kV, while megawatt-scale projects require 33kV, 66kV, or 110kV interconnection. Each voltage level carries different technical requirements and cost implications, which the feasibility study outlines.

Interconnection cost estimation includes transmission line construction, substation upgrades if needed, and utility coordination expenses. For remote sites, these costs can exceed several crores, potentially rendering otherwise attractive sites economically unviable. The study provides preliminary cost ranges, allowing developers to factor these expenses into financial models.

Utility coordination requirements vary by state and distribution company (DISCOM). The feasibility study identifies the relevant utility, notes their interconnection policies, and estimates approval timelines. Some states offer streamlined processes, while others involve lengthy bureaucratic procedures that impact project schedules.

## The Complete Feasibility Study Process

Understanding the step-by-step feasibility study process helps EPC companies plan their project timelines and prepare necessary inputs. While specific methodologies vary among service providers, the fundamental workflow remains consistent across the industry.

The process begins with initial client consultation and requirement gathering. Engineers discuss project objectives, capacity targets, budget constraints, and timeline expectations. Clients provide available site information including location coordinates, land documents, any existing surveys, and preliminary project concepts. This phase establishes scope and ensures alignment between client expectations and deliverable content.

Next comes desktop analysis and preliminary assessment. Using satellite imagery, GIS data, and publicly available information, engineers conduct remote site evaluation. They analyze solar resource data, identify nearby substations, review topographical maps, and assess preliminary layout options. This desktop phase, typically completed in 2-3 days, provides initial viability indicators before committing to site visits.

The site visit and data collection phase involves physical inspection by qualified engineers. Teams document site conditions through photographs and videos, verify land boundaries, identify obstacles and shading sources, assess soil conditions, and measure distances to key infrastructure. In India, site visits for ground-mount projects often reveal conditions not visible in satellite imagery, such as seasonal water logging, unauthorized encroachments, or access restrictions.

Following data collection, engineers proceed to technical analysis and modeling. Using specialized software, they create preliminary system layouts, run shading simulations, calculate energy generation estimates, and model different configuration options. This phase represents the analytical heart of the feasibility study, where raw data transforms into actionable insights.

Financial viability assessment translates technical findings into economic projections. Engineers estimate capital costs, project operational expenses, calculate revenue based on applicable tariffs or power purchase agreements, and compute key financial metrics. This analysis helps stakeholders understand not just whether the project can be built, but whether it should be built from a business perspective.

Finally, report compilation and presentation packages all findings into a comprehensive document. The report synthesizes technical analysis, financial projections, risk assessments, and recommendations into an executive summary suitable for decision-makers. Many service providers, including Heaven Designs, also conduct presentation meetings to walk clients through findings and answer questions.

## Feasibility Study Timeline: What to Expect

For solar EPC companies planning project schedules, understanding typical feasibility study timelines helps coordinate subsequent activities. While duration varies based on project complexity and site accessibility, most studies follow a predictable timeline structure.

The standard feasibility study timeline ranges from 7 to 14 days from project kickoff to final report delivery. This timeframe assumes reasonable site accessibility, availability of basic site data, and normal working conditions. Complex projects involving multiple sites, difficult terrain, or extensive grid analysis may extend to 3 weeks.

Breaking down the timeline by phase, desktop analysis typically requires 2-3 days. Engineers gather satellite data, analyze solar resources, review available documentation, and prepare for site visits. This phase can proceed quickly when clients provide comprehensive initial information.

The site survey phase usually takes 1-2 days, depending on site size and location. A 10-acre rooftop site in an urban area might require just half a day, while a 100-acre ground-mount site in a remote location could need two full days. Travel time to distant sites adds to this duration. For projects across India, Heaven Designs coordinates site survey services efficiently to minimize timeline impact.

Analysis and modeling represents the longest phase, typically requiring 3-5 days. Engineers process collected data, create system layouts, run multiple simulation scenarios, perform shading analysis, and develop financial models. The complexity of this phase depends on site characteristics and the number of design alternatives evaluated.

Report preparation takes 2-3 days as engineers compile findings, create visualizations, write recommendations, and format deliverables. Quality service providers include internal review processes to ensure accuracy before client delivery.

Several factors can affect the feasibility study timeline. Site accessibility issues, such as monsoon flooding or restricted access, may delay site visits. Incomplete initial data requires additional information gathering. Complex grid connectivity scenarios involving multiple utilities or voltage levels extend analysis time. Client responsiveness for clarifications and approvals also impacts overall duration.

Understanding how timeline impacts project planning helps EPCs schedule subsequent activities. The feasibility study should be completed before finalizing land acquisition, as findings may reveal deal-breakers. It must precede detailed engineering procurement, since the study defines scope and system configuration. For time-sensitive projects with regulatory deadlines or seasonal construction windows, expedited feasibility studies can sometimes be completed in 5-7 days, though this requires premium coordination and resource allocation.

## Key Deliverables in a Feasibility Study Report

A comprehensive feasibility study report delivers multiple components, each serving specific decision-making needs. Understanding these deliverables helps EPC companies evaluate service provider proposals and ensure they receive complete analysis.

The executive summary provides a concise overview with a clear go/no-go recommendation. This section, typically 2-3 pages, summarizes key findings, highlights critical risks or opportunities, states recommended system capacity and configuration, and provides high-level financial metrics. Busy executives often read only this section, making its clarity and completeness essential.

Site layout and preliminary design deliverables include scaled site plans showing proposed panel placement, access roads, inverter locations, and electrical routing. While not construction-ready, these layouts demonstrate spatial feasibility and provide a visual reference for stakeholders. Many reports include 3D renderings or aerial visualizations that help non-technical stakeholders understand the proposed installation.

Energy generation estimates form the technical core of the feasibility study. Reports specify annual energy production in kWh or MWh, monthly generation profiles accounting for seasonal variations, performance ratio assumptions and loss calculations, and specific yield (kWh per kWp installed). These projections, typically provided with confidence ranges (P50, P75, P90), enable financial modeling and revenue forecasting.

Financial viability metrics translate technical performance into business outcomes. Key metrics include project IRR (Internal Rate of Return), NPV (Net Present Value), simple payback period, levelized cost of energy (LCOE), and return on investment over the project lifetime. The report should clearly state assumptions regarding capital costs, O&M expenses, tariff rates, and discount rates used in calculations.

Risk assessment and mitigation strategies identify potential project challenges and propose solutions. Common risks documented in Indian solar feasibility studies include grid curtailment possibilities, land acquisition complications, regulatory approval uncertainties, equipment availability and pricing volatility, and environmental or social concerns. For each identified risk, the report should suggest mitigation approaches and estimate probability and impact.

Technical specifications and equipment recommendations provide preliminary guidance on system components. While detailed equipment selection occurs during engineering design, the feasibility study recommends module types and wattages, inverter configurations (string vs. central), mounting structure approaches (fixed-tilt vs. tracking), and electrical architecture (AC vs. DC coupling for hybrid systems). These recommendations establish the baseline for subsequent detailed engineering.

Finally, the report outlines next steps and detailed engineering scope. It defines what additional work is required to move from feasibility to construction-ready design, estimates timelines for detailed engineering and permitting, identifies critical path activities, and may provide cost estimates for subsequent project phases. This roadmap helps EPCs plan resources and budgets for project advancement.

## Financial Viability Metrics in Feasibility Studies

Financial analysis within a feasibility study determines whether a technically viable project makes business sense. Understanding these metrics helps EPC companies and their clients make informed investment decisions.

Capital expenditure (CAPEX) estimation provides preliminary project cost projections. For Indian solar projects in 2026, typical CAPEX ranges from ₹35-45 lakhs per MW for ground-mount systems and ₹40-50 lakhs per MW for rooftop installations, though costs vary based on location, scale, and specifications. The feasibility study breaks down CAPEX into major categories: solar modules, inverters and electrical equipment, mounting structures, civil and foundation work, grid connectivity and transmission, and engineering, procurement, and construction margins.

Operational expenditure (OPEX) projections estimate ongoing costs throughout the project’s 25-year lifespan. Annual O&M typically ranges from 0.5-1.5% of CAPEX for well-maintained systems. Components include preventive and corrective maintenance, module cleaning (particularly important in dusty Indian conditions), security and site management, insurance premiums, land lease payments if applicable, and inverter replacements at 10-12 year intervals.

Levelized cost of energy (LCOE) calculations provide a standardized metric for comparing project economics. LCOE represents the per-unit cost of electricity generation over the project lifetime, accounting for all capital and operating expenses. In India, competitive solar projects achieve LCOE between ₹2.50-3.50 per kWh, depending on location, scale, and financing terms. The feasibility study calculates LCOE using standard formulas and compares it to applicable tariffs or grid electricity costs.

Return on investment (ROI) analysis examines profitability from multiple angles. Internal Rate of Return (IRR) indicates the discount rate at which NPV equals zero, with most Indian solar projects targeting 12-18% IRR depending on risk profile. Net Present Value (NPV) shows absolute profit in today’s rupees, helping compare projects of different scales. Equity IRR, relevant for leveraged projects, measures returns specifically on equity investment after accounting for debt service.

Payback period calculations indicate how quickly the project recovers initial investment. Simple payback, which doesn’t account for time value of money, typically ranges from 5-8 years for Indian solar projects. Discounted payback, which applies discount rates, extends to 7-10 years. These metrics help stakeholders understand liquidity and risk exposure timelines.

Sensitivity analysis tests how financial outcomes change when key variables fluctuate. The feasibility study typically examines sensitivity to module efficiency and degradation rates, tariff or power purchase agreement rates, debt interest rates and terms, O&M cost variations, and grid availability or curtailment scenarios. This analysis identifies which variables most significantly impact returns, guiding risk management focus.

## How Feasibility Studies Prevent Costly Mistakes

The true value of a feasibility study often lies not in what it confirms, but in what it prevents. By identifying problems early, these studies save solar EPC companies from expensive mistakes that would only become apparent after significant investment.

Identifying site constraints before detailed engineering represents the primary value proposition. A thorough feasibility study reveals issues like excessive shading that would reduce generation below viable levels, soil conditions requiring expensive foundation solutions, flood zones or drainage problems affecting equipment placement, and inadequate land area for target capacity. Discovering these constraints during feasibility, when only lakhs have been invested, prevents wasting tens of lakhs on detailed engineering for an ultimately unviable site.

Avoiding unsuitable locations early saves not just engineering costs but also land acquisition expenses. Consider a real scenario from a Gujarat-based EPC: they identified a 50-acre site for a 10 MW project, negotiated land lease terms, and were preparing to finalize agreements when a feasibility study revealed the nearest substation had zero evacuation capacity, with the next viable connection point 12 km away. Transmission costs would have exceeded ₹2 crores, destroying project economics. The study, costing under ₹2 lakhs, prevented a disastrous commitment.

Preventing grid connectivity surprises protects project timelines and budgets. In many Indian states, grid connection approvals involve complex bureaucratic processes and technical requirements that vary by utility. A feasibility study engages with relevant DISCOMs early, identifies specific requirements, estimates realistic approval timelines, and uncovers potential roadblocks like required substation upgrades or transmission line construction. This foresight allows accurate project scheduling and cost estimation.

Optimizing system sizing and configuration ensures projects are neither over-designed (wasting capital) nor under-designed (missing revenue opportunities). The feasibility study determines optimal capacity based on land constraints, grid connection limits, and financial returns. It evaluates trade-offs between fixed-tilt and tracking systems, assesses whether bifacial modules justify their premium cost, and determines ideal inverter loading ratios. These optimization decisions, made during feasibility, establish the foundation for efficient detailed engineering.

Real-world examples illustrate the cost-saving impact. A Maharashtra-based developer commissioned a feasibility study for a proposed 5 MW rooftop project across multiple industrial buildings. The study revealed that three of the seven buildings had structural capacity insufficient for solar loads without expensive reinforcement. By eliminating those buildings and redesigning for 3.2 MW across the viable structures, the developer avoided ₹40+ lakhs in structural upgrades while still achieving acceptable project returns.

Another case involved a Rajasthan ground-mount project where the feasibility study identified seasonal water logging in a low-lying portion of the site. By adjusting the layout to avoid that zone and incorporating proper drainage, the study prevented what would have been recurring equipment damage and generation losses. The cost of the feasibility study: ₹1.5 lakhs. The estimated cost of addressing water damage after construction: ₹15+ lakhs over the project lifetime.

The financial logic is compelling. A comprehensive feasibility study typically costs 0.5-1.5% of total project CAPEX. Yet it routinely identifies issues that, if unaddressed, would cost 5-20% of CAPEX to remediate or would reduce project returns by similar magnitudes. This 10:1 or better return on investment makes feasibility studies one of the most cost-effective risk management tools available to solar EPCs.

## Feasibility Study Requirements for Indian Solar Projects

India’s diverse regulatory landscape means feasibility study requirements vary significantly across states and project types. Understanding these variations ensures studies address all necessary compliance aspects.

State-specific regulatory requirements create a complex compliance environment. Some states mandate feasibility studies for projects above certain capacity thresholds, while others leave this to developer discretion. States like Gujarat and Rajasthan, with mature solar markets, have streamlined processes and clear guidelines. Emerging markets like Bihar or Jharkhand may have less defined requirements, requiring more extensive regulatory research during the feasibility study.

MNRE guidelines and compliance establish national-level standards, particularly for projects seeking central government subsidies or incentives. The Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) specifies technical standards for system design, equipment specifications, and performance expectations. A thorough feasibility study verifies compliance with these guidelines, ensuring projects remain eligible for applicable incentive schemes.

Environmental clearance considerations vary based on project size and location. Projects on forest land, near protected areas, or above certain capacity thresholds require environmental impact assessments. The feasibility study identifies whether environmental clearances are needed, estimates the approval timeline (which can extend 6-12 months), and flags potential environmental concerns that might complicate approvals.

Land use regulations across Indian states impose different restrictions on solar development. Agricultural land conversion requires specific approvals in most states, with varying difficulty and timelines. Some states restrict solar development in certain zones or require minimum distances from residential areas. The feasibility study researches applicable land use regulations and confirms the proposed site complies or identifies required approvals.

Grid code compliance requirements dictate technical specifications for grid-connected systems. State electricity regulatory commissions publish grid codes specifying voltage and frequency operating ranges, power quality requirements, reactive power capabilities, and protection and metering standards. The feasibility study ensures the proposed system design can meet these technical requirements, preventing costly redesigns during detailed engineering.

Documentation needed for project approvals varies by jurisdiction but typically includes land ownership documents, site survey reports, single-line diagrams, technical specifications, and financial viability assessments. The feasibility study identifies exactly which documents are required for the specific project location and type, allowing EPCs to begin preparation early. This proactive approach prevents approval delays that could jeopardize project timelines or financing commitments.
